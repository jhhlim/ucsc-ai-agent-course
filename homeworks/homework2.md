## Homework 2 - multi-agent application

### Goals
* Develop a practical and fun multi-agent system
* Make multiple decisions, use at least 1 external tool
* Incorporate a "critic agent" pattern

### Homework ideas
Below is a list of ideas, pick one resonates with you or come up with your own idea.

#### Presentation Generator
Build an agent that produces a polished slide deck from a topic prompt and audience description. The agent must plan the deck structure, research content, select or generate visuals, and assemble the final presentation. A critic agent reviews each draft for clarity, narrative flow, and audience appropriateness, sending revision feedback until the deck meets quality standards or a maximum iteration count is reached. Students will see firsthand how the worker-critic loop dramatically improves output quality compared to single-shot generation.

#### High-level requirements. 
The agent must make at least three meaningful decisions: deck structure and slide count, content depth per slide, and visual choices. It must use at least one external tool (image search, web research for content, or a PPTX generation library counts if it involves real API calls). It must recover from at least one failure, such as image search returning no results, content sources being unavailable, or the slide generator rejecting malformed input. A critic agent must review at least one dimension of quality (suggested: narrative coherence) and the worker must demonstrably revise based on feedback. Deliverables include the agent code, three generated decks on different topics, and full worker-critic transcripts.

#### Personal Finance Explainer
Build an agent that helps users understand financial concepts and compare concrete options without giving advice on what to do. The agent handles two modes: explaining concepts ("how does an APR work?") with worked examples, and comparing user-supplied options ("Loan A vs. Loan B with these terms") by computing actual numbers and surfacing tradeoffs. The hard rule is that the agent never tells users what to choose — it explains, calculates, and lays out considerations. A critic agent enforces both accuracy and scope, catching mathematical errors and any drift into advice-giving.

#### High-level requirements. 
The agent must make at least three decisions: which concepts need explaining versus assumed known, which numbers to compute precisely versus describe qualitatively, and which tradeoffs are worth surfacing. It must use at least one external tool, with a code-execution or calculator tool strongly recommended since LLMs are unreliable at compound interest math. Failure recovery must handle at least one of: incomplete user-provided numbers, out-of-scope advice requests, or factual misconceptions in the user's question. The critic agent must verify numerical accuracy (ideally by re-running calculations) and enforce the no-advice constraint. Deliverables include the agent, transcripts of five provided test scenarios, and a short reflection on cases where the critic caught advice-drift.

#### Study Buddy Agent
Build an agent that tutors a student in a specific subject (you assign the subject — intro biology, introductory statistics, or similar) by generating practice questions, evaluating answers, and adapting difficulty based on performance. The agent must decide what to ask next, how to respond to wrong answers (explain, give a hint, or move on), and when the student has mastered a topic. A critic agent reviews each generated question for factual accuracy and difficulty calibration before it reaches the student, catching the kinds of subtle errors that LLMs frequently make in technical subjects.

#### High-level requirements. 
The agent must make at least three decisions: question topic and difficulty selection, response strategy to wrong answers (hint vs. explanation vs. simpler question), and mastery determination. It must use at least one external tool — Wikipedia API, a textbook excerpt retrieval system, or a domain-specific reference. Failure recovery must handle ambiguous student answers, repeated incorrect responses, or external source lookups returning irrelevant material. The critic agent must verify factual accuracy of generated questions and flag mis-calibrated difficulty. Deliverables include the agent, a transcript of a 15-minute tutoring session, and an analysis of three cases where the critic improved question quality.

#### Smart Grocery Budget Agent
Build an agent that produces a weekly grocery shopping list optimized for a given budget, household size, and dietary preferences. The agent must select items, make substitutions when needed, and explain its tradeoffs. A critic agent plays a household member with strong opinions (the "picky spouse" persona works well) and pushes back on bland or repetitive choices, forcing the worker to justify decisions and revise. The adversarial critic teaches students that critic personas with strong viewpoints produce sharper feedback than neutral reviewers.

#### High-level requirements. 
The agent must make at least three decisions: item selection within budget, substitution choices when items are unavailable, and tradeoff resolution (more variety vs. cheaper, healthier vs. tastier). It must use at least one external tool — a price/inventory API (real or mocked via JSON), a nutrition data API, or a recipe search. Failure recovery must handle at least one of: out-of-stock items, budget overrun, or dietary conflict (e.g., user said "vegetarian" but the cheapest protein source is chicken). The critic agent must push back on at least one dimension (suggested: meal variety or nutritional balance) with a clear persona. Deliverables include the agent, three sample shopping lists for different household profiles, and worker-critic transcripts showing meaningful revision.

#### Fake News Claim Investigator
Build an agent that investigates a viral claim and produces a structured report — not a true/false verdict. The agent takes a claim as input and produces sections covering what the claim asserts, what credible sources say, where disagreements exist, what context is missing, and how settled the matter is (e.g., "well-established," "actively disputed," "insufficient evidence"). A critic agent plays a skeptical editor who catches unsupported assertions in the report, over-reliance on single sources, missing counter-evidence, and language that sneaks in a verdict. This assignment teaches students that good agent design sometimes means producing structured uncertainty rather than confident answers.

#### High-level requirements. 
The agent must make at least three decisions: which sub-claims within the input are worth investigating, which sources are credible enough to cite, and when investigation is sufficiently complete. It must use at least one external tool — web search and web fetch are the natural fits, with Wikipedia API or news-archive APIs as alternatives. Failure recovery must handle at least one of: contradicting sources, dead or paywalled links, or vague claims that need clarification before investigation. The critic agent must check epistemic quality — specifically whether report claims are actually supported by cited sources and whether verdict-language has slipped in. Deliverables include the agent, investigations of five claims you provide (mix of easy consensus, genuinely disputed, definitional, partially-true, and vague), and the critic-worker transcripts.

#### Red Team Resume
Build an agent that helps a user strengthen their resume for a specific job posting. The worker agent edits and tailors the resume; an adversarial critic agent plays a hostile hiring manager who attacks every weak claim, vague accomplishment, and buzzword. The worker must defend or revise based on each critique. This assignment is the clearest demonstration of how critic persona design changes outcomes — a polite critic catches little, while a hostile one forces real improvement.

#### High-level requirements. 
The agent must make at least three decisions: which resume weaknesses to address first, how to rephrase weak claims with stronger evidence, and which job posting requirements to emphasize. It must use at least one external tool — web fetch for the job posting URL, or a skill/keyword extraction service. Failure recovery must handle at least one of: a dead job posting URL, a resume in an unparseable format, or a job posting with vague requirements that need inference. The critic agent must have an explicit adversarial persona and produce specific, attackable critiques rather than generic feedback. Deliverables include the agent, before/after versions of two resumes against two different job postings, full critic-worker transcripts, and a short reflection comparing the adversarial critic's output to what a "polite reviewer" critic would have produced (students must run both for one case).

#### Grocery-to-Meal-Plan Agent
Build an agent that takes a list of available ingredients (what's in the user's fridge and pantry) and produces a meal plan covering several days, minimizing waste and respecting dietary constraints. The agent must select recipes, allocate ingredients across meals, identify the small set of additional items that need to be purchased, and handle dietary restrictions. A critic agent plays a nutritionist checking macronutrient balance and meal variety, sending the worker back when the plan is unbalanced or repetitive.

#### High-level requirements. 
The agent must make at least three decisions: recipe selection from available ingredients, ingredient allocation across meals to avoid waste, and supplementary shopping list generation. It must use at least one external tool — a recipe search API, nutrition database, or ingredient-substitution reference. Failure recovery must handle at least one of: insufficient ingredients for a complete plan, dietary conflicts with available items, or recipe lookups returning unsuitable results. The critic agent must evaluate at least one substantive dimension (nutritional balance, variety, or feasibility) with enough specificity that the worker can meaningfully revise. Deliverables include the agent, three meal plans generated from different starting pantries, the resulting shopping lists, and the critic-worker transcripts.
